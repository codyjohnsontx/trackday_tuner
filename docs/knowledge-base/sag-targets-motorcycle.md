title: Motorcycle Sag Targets
tags: [sag, baseline, motorcycle]
---

There is no universal perfect sag number for every motorcycle, rider, tire, and track. Still, common baseline windows help you decide whether you are in a workable range before chasing damping.

A practical starting point for many track motorcycles is roughly 30 to 40 mm of front rider sag and around 25 to 35 mm of rear rider sag. Free sag often lands in a smaller band, but the exact target depends on spring rate, linkage ratio, and how the bike is meant to steer.

Use these numbers as a starting window, not a rule. If the bike is stable, turning well, and driving off the corner, do not throw away a good setup because it is slightly outside a generic range. The real value of the measurement is consistency and trend tracking.

If front rider sag is very large, the bike may ride low on the nose and feel lazy or vague on entry and mid-corner. If front sag is very small, the bike may feel nervous or harsh and can skate over bumps instead of tracking through them. Similar logic applies at the rear: too much rear sag can make the bike squat and run wide, while too little can reduce drive grip and make the rear feel tall or nervous.

When you adjust preload to change sag, record the new measurement and the rider's feedback after the session. The stored combination of sag plus feel is usually more valuable than the number by itself.
