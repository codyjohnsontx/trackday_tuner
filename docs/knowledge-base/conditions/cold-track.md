# Cold-track conditions

Cold track temperatures reduce grip and slow heat build-up. Setup that was perfect in a hot session is almost never perfect in a cold one.

## Tires

- Grip comes in later and leaves sooner between laps. Treat the first lap of each session as a warm-up, not a flying lap.
- Hot pressures will be lower than normal because the tire is not working as hard. Expect a smaller cold-to-hot delta. Lowering cold pressures 1-2 psi brings hot pressures back toward target.
- Do not chase grip with big pressure changes until you have run at least one session and measured hot pressures.

## Suspension

A cold track usually reduces mechanical grip and tire deflection. Damping changes are usually unnecessary; softer compression can help initial turn-in grip but risks instability later in the lap if the track warms.

## Rider and driver factors

Body and brain warm up slower on cold days. Build pace gradually. Small inputs and smooth throttle produce better times than aggressive inputs on cold tires.

## When to change setup

If the forecast is for a cold day, bias the cold pressures down 1-2 psi and keep other setup the same as your last similar-temperature log. Let the pressures guide you before changing dampers or geometry.
