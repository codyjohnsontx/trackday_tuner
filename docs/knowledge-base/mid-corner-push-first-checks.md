title: Mid-Corner Push First Checks
tags: [push, handling, diagnosis]
---

When the bike pushes mid-corner, the first job is to slow down the diagnosis and avoid making five guesses at once. Mid-corner push can come from front tire pressure, ride height balance, excessive rear squat, too much fork preload, too little front support, or even a line and timing issue from the rider.

A clean first-check sequence is practical. Confirm the front tire pressure is in the expected working range. Look at sag and general ride attitude to make sure the chassis is not starting too low or too high at one end. Review any recent rear ride-height or preload changes, because a taller or lower rear can strongly shift mid-corner balance.

Then check the session history. If the same track and bike felt better before, the fastest answer is often in the stored setup deltas rather than in theory. A previous session that turned well with slightly different pressures or ride height is better evidence than generic advice.

Do not jump straight to damping unless a specific symptom points there. Riders often call multiple front-end issues "push." If the bike is actually vague on the brake release, running wide because the rear is squatting, or standing up from line errors, a damping change may not fix the root cause.

Best practice is one small change at a time. Record what you changed, what the rider felt, and whether the push improved at corner entry, mid-corner, or exit. Those distinctions matter.
