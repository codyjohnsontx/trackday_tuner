# Compound and wear

Compound choice sets the operating temperature window and peak grip of the tire. Wear state shifts that window as the tire ages.

## Compound selection

Softer compounds warm up faster and reach higher peak grip but overheat sooner and wear faster. Harder compounds take longer to come in and have a lower peak, but hold up for many sessions.

- Cold track, short session: bias softer.
- Hot track, long session or back-to-back runs: bias harder.
- Uncertain: pick the compound that matches your pace on this tire in similar conditions from your log.

## Wear state effects

A new tire has full tread depth, maximum heat capacity, and slightly lower initial grip until scrubbed. A scrubbed tire is the typical sweet spot. A used tire has reduced thermal mass and a narrower window. A worn tire is heat-sensitive and should be pressure-managed carefully.

## Interactions with pressure

Worn tires often need slightly lower cold pressures than new tires, because less rubber means less internal friction and a smaller cold-to-hot delta. If a set that felt great new now feels greasy, try dropping 0.5-1.0 psi cold before blaming the setup.

## Session pacing

If a compound is overheating, a cool-down lap or a longer gap between sessions often recovers grip better than a pressure change. Treat pressure as the last lever when thermal management can solve it.
