title: Tire Tearing Basics
tags: [tires, tearing, pressure]
---

Tire tearing is a useful clue, but only when you connect it to temperature, pressure, and how the bike is working the carcass. Trackside, the most practical first checks are hot pressure, track temperature, pace, and whether the tire has already been through too many heat cycles.

Cold tearing and hot tearing are often discussed like clear categories, but the main point for a rider is simpler: the tire surface is showing that the load, temperature, and slip pattern are not working together well. Sometimes that means pressure is wrong. Sometimes the suspension is not controlling load consistently. Sometimes the tire is just beyond its best life.

If tearing appears, check the pressure against the tire vendor's current recommendation first. Then review whether a suspension or ride-height change was made at the same time. If the bike became harsher or less compliant, the tire may be doing more work than before.

Logs matter here because tearing often shows up after a chain of small changes. A rider may only remember the last one. Stored session notes make it easier to see whether the issue began after a rebound adjustment, a geometry change, or a hotter afternoon session on the same tire.

Treat tire wear as data, not just damage. Write down what the surface looked like, how the bike felt, the pressure used, and whether the problem improved after the next adjustment.
