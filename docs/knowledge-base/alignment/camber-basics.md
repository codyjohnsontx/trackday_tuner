# Camber basics (car)

Camber is the tilt of the tire relative to vertical, viewed from the front. Negative camber tilts the top of the tire inward. Most track cars run negative camber on all four corners.

## Why negative camber

When a car rolls in a corner, the outside tire rolls onto its edge. Static negative camber compensates for roll so the contact patch lands flat at maximum load. Too little camber and the tire rolls onto its outside edge (understeer and outside-edge wear). Too much camber and the tire rides on its inside edge even at full load (reduced contact and inside-edge wear).

## Typical ranges

Consult your chassis and tire supplier first. General trackday ranges on street-based cars:

- Front: -2.5 to -3.5 degrees.
- Rear: -1.5 to -2.5 degrees.

Dedicated track cars often run more. Street-driven cars must stay within tire-wear and handling tolerances for normal use.

## Reading tire wear

Check tire temperatures across the tread (inside, middle, outside) after a full session. A reasonably even spread within 10-15 C indicates camber is close. A hot inside edge with a cool outside edge suggests too much negative camber. A cool inside with a hot outside suggests not enough.

## Interaction with toe

Camber and toe are coupled. A toe change can mask or amplify a camber issue. Make one change at a time and re-measure.
