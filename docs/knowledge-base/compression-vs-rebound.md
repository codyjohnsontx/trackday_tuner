---
id: compression-vs-rebound
title: Compression Versus Rebound
tags: [compression, rebound, suspension]
---

Compression damping and rebound damping are easy to mix up because both affect how controlled the bike feels. The difference is the direction of movement. Compression damping acts when the suspension is moving into the stroke. Rebound damping acts when it is extending back out.

Compression changes are often noticed during braking zones, curb strikes, big bump hits, and initial squat or dive. Too much compression can feel harsh, chattery, or unwilling to absorb bumps. Too little compression can make the bike blow through the stroke and feel loose or under-supported.

Rebound changes show up in recovery. Too much rebound can make the suspension return too slowly, causing the bike to pack down through repeated bumps or transitions. Too little rebound can make the chassis spring back too quickly and feel bouncy or unsettled after load release.

If the rider says the front feels harsh over braking bumps, compression is a sensible first check. If the rider says the bike does one movement and then continues to wobble or spring back too quickly, rebound is a stronger suspect. If the bike feels like it never quite recovers between corners, too much rebound is often worth checking.

Keep changes small and write down both the click position and the symptom you were chasing. Without that record, it becomes hard to tell whether the change solved the right problem or simply moved the feeling somewhere else.
