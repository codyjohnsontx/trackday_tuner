# Gearing basics (motorcycle)

Gearing changes how engine RPM maps to wheel speed. On a trackday motorcycle, the usual goal is to land useful gears for the corners of the specific track.

## Front vs rear sprocket

- One tooth less on the front = shorter gearing (more acceleration, lower top speed). Roughly equivalent to about three teeth more on the rear.
- One tooth more on the rear = shorter gearing. Much finer adjustment than the front.
- One tooth more on the front = taller gearing. Rare in track use.

Most trackday gearing changes are done on the rear sprocket because it is cheap, fast, and allows finer steps.

## Choosing gearing

- Second gear corner that drops RPM below the powerband: shorten gearing.
- Tapping the rev limiter in top gear on the main straight: lengthen gearing, but only if you are not losing time in corners.
- Lap time is the truth. A faster motorcycle on the straight that falls out of the powerband mid-corner will lap slower.

## Chain length

Changing the rear sprocket by more than 2 teeth usually requires a different chain length or axle position. Check swingarm adjustment range before ordering.

## Log what you ran

Record the sprocket combo every session. Gearing that felt perfect at one track is a useful starting point when you return, and a starting reference when you go to a new track of similar length.
