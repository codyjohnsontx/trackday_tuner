# Change one thing at a time

The single most important setup discipline is changing one thing at a time. This is how you learn what your vehicle does, what your tires do, and what your inputs do.

## Why it matters

If you change two things and the bike/car feels better, you do not know which change helped. You also do not know whether the changes fought each other and the net is less improvement than either alone. Stacking changes compounds uncertainty session over session.

## Exceptions

There are cases where two changes are coupled (for example, raising rear ride height and compensating with a small damper change to avoid packing). When you must change two things, log both and treat the result as a combined data point, not two independent ones.

## Session structure

1. Pick one change you believe will address the clearest symptom from the last session.
2. Predict what you expect to feel in the next session before riding/driving.
3. Run a full session at representative pace.
4. Compare what you felt to what you predicted. Log both.

This prediction habit is what separates fast improvement from random churn.

## Reverting

If a change makes things worse, revert before the next session. Do not stack another change hoping to cancel the first. Get back to a known-good baseline, then try a different hypothesis.
