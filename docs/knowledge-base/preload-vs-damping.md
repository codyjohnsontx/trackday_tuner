---
id: preload-vs-damping
title: Preload Versus Damping
tags: [preload, damping, suspension]
---

Preload and damping do different jobs, even though riders often feel both through support and chassis movement. Preload changes how much force is already applied to the spring at rest. Damping controls how fast the suspension moves once a load is applied.

If you add preload, you are not making the spring rate stiffer. You are changing where the bike sits in the travel and how much force it takes to start moving from that position. This is why preload is often the first lever for setting sag and ride attitude.

Damping is the control system. Compression damping slows the suspension as it compresses into the stroke. Rebound damping slows it as it extends back out. If preload is badly off, damping changes can feel confusing because the bike is starting from the wrong part of the stroke.

A good practical rule is this: use preload to establish the operating window, then use damping to control the speed of movement inside that window. If the bike is diving too far, squatting too much, or riding obviously too high or low, check sag and preload first. If the bike is oscillating, recovering too quickly, packing down, or feeling harsh over repeated bumps, then damping is the more likely next area to inspect.

Trackside, avoid changing both preload and damping at the same time unless the current setup is obviously wrong. When the rider changes one variable at a time, the feedback becomes easier to trust and future sessions become easier to compare.
