title: Chatter Diagnosis Basics
tags: [chatter, diagnosis, suspension]
---

Chatter is one of the easiest symptoms to misread because riders use the word for different sensations. A real first step is to define where it happens: braking, mid-corner, edge grip, or exit drive. Without that context, advice becomes vague quickly.

Chatter often points to a tire and suspension interaction problem rather than one single clicker. Tire pressure, carcass condition, temperature, ride height, spring support, and damping can all contribute. Because of that, the first checks should be broad but controlled.

Start with tire condition and pressure. Then review the last known good setup. If chatter appeared right after a preload, ride height, or damping change, reverse one step before inventing a new theory. Repeated comparisons to prior sessions are especially valuable here because chatter usually feels different from lap to lap depending on temperature and surface grip.

If the front chatters at lean, check whether the bike is overloaded on the nose or lacking compliance. If the rear chatters on exit, check whether the tire is overheating or the suspension is not following the track surface well. A harsh bike that skips is a different problem from a soft bike that moves too much.

The safe trackside process is to identify where the chatter is happening, confirm tire basics, compare to the last stable session, and then make one controlled change. Chatter diagnosis gets worse, not better, when three adjustments are stacked with no notes.
