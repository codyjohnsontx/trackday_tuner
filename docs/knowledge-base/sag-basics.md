title: Sag Basics
tags: [sag, suspension, baseline]
---

Sag is how much the suspension compresses from fully extended length once the bike or car is sitting on its wheels. The two most common numbers are free sag and rider sag. Free sag compares topped-out length to the bike-only number. Rider sag compares topped-out length to the loaded number with the rider in normal gear.

The practical reason sag matters is simple: it sets where the suspension sits in its travel before the track starts adding load. Too much sag can leave the bike low in the stroke, soft on support, and vague on direction change. Too little sag can leave the chassis riding too high, harsh over bumps, and short on grip when the tire needs compliance.

Sag is a baseline, not a magic number. It tells you whether spring preload is putting the machine in a workable starting window. If sag is far off, damping changes often become confusing because the spring and ride-height foundation is not settled first.

At the track, use sag to answer two questions. First: is the spring/preload combination in a usable range? Second: if the rider reports vague front feel, poor edge grip, or a bike that stands up and runs wide, is the chassis starting too low or too high in the travel?

Measure carefully, record the numbers, and treat them as setup references. If the bike feels better later in the day, the stored sag numbers help you trace what changed instead of guessing from memory.
